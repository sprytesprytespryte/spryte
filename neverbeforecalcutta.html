The “Black Hole of Calcutta” narrative grew from a desire for emotional British propaganda which could bleed into future culture. As a motif it appears in literature and journalism throughout the Victorian era and runs alongside Britain’s rule over India like an ideological support system for maintaining imperialistic momentum. The construction of a national trauma can bode well for colonial expansion and the success in these efforts shows in the re-sprouting of derogatory concepts and tropes across time and historical media. Dicke’s proposal speaks volumes: to re-engage the name of a controversial event which was and still is widely contested is a prime example of how the way in which we describe objects can ultimately extend problematic narratives, hidden not-so-deep in the linguistic undercurrents until they resurface with a new topology.

The following timeline is a study into the etymology of “black hole”, along with some more tangential connections which help to inform of certain actors and situations that facilitate the trajectory of the term.

It is clear why “black hole” is deemed an appropriate name for describing this type of astronomical body without much further consideration: it signifies a black area which emits no light, where anything that travels across its boundary must fall in. And yet, it is exactly this strong compatibility which means that the story behind the term is unknown, or worse dismissed. It has such syntactical success that it allows for this new astrophysical manifestation of a “black hole” to hold ground and even become embedded into areas of international communication such as economic journalism, to help express the financial instability of national economies and football clubs. This exponential evolution into spectacle propels forward ideas and visuals quickly enough to dismiss their significant origins.

1686

The East India Trading Company (EIC), a British-owned joint-stock business, has been trading in India since the start of the century. Although a private corporation, it receives diplomatic and financial aid from the British Crown as it grow relationships with Indian royalty to gain trade benefits, such as waived tax duties. By this point in time, King Charles II has awarded EIC with certain powers to significantly increase their autonomy overseas: the right to acquire territory, coin their own money, hold military leadership over forts and troops, privately produce alliances and conflicts and exercise both civil and criminal jurisdiction in the communities that they build.

EIC travels to the north-east of India to create a new settlement and expand its trade lines. Along the eastern bank of Hooghly River in Bengal (a region referred to by the Mughals as “the paradise of nations”), they occupy and merge three existing villages into a singular territory, using the central village Kalikata to officially rename the area Calcutta. The city is soon surrounded by settlements built by other European traders, such as the French in Chandernagor and eventually the Danish in Serampore. Further out, the Portuguese hold a commercial monopoly along the mainland’s western coast, whilst the Dutch create several outposts along the southern tip.

1704
Historian Swati Chattopadhyay argues that this distinction is misleading and mere extension of the Eurocentric narrative: “in Bengali parlance there was no equivalent to the white town/black town duality … the city was divided into a host of paras, tolas and tulis … although these localities did not have fixed boundaries or legal bearing, they formed a block that residents could identify with”.

Calcutta has 30,000 inhabitants. The city attempts to be structurally segregated, with British and Europeans living in the supposedly more civilised and considered “White Town” while Indians and other Asians are assigned to living in the less desirable “Black Town”.

The city develops in an area where the Hindu deity worshipped by local followers is Kali, the Goddess of Destruction, Time and Power. The Kali Kalighat Temple nearby the city attracts constant footfall of devotees.
